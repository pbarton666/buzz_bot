PES has substantial expertise working with consumer products companies besides having experience in DSM.

Why is this important?
Consumer products are tough. Firms must sell discretionary products that are virtually indistinguishable from their rivals', usually into a very competitive, low-margin markets. Face it, who really needs Coke? And how does one brand of really differ from the next?
Utilities are fundamentally engineering organizations, great at manufacturing and reliably delivering a potentially-dangerous product to millions of homes, ... but in a largely captive market. In terms of culture and raw "corporate DNA" they tend not be emphasize marketing.
Traditional approaches like the use of conservation supply curves and implied discount rates applied against demographically-derived market segments tend to address the question of "what people ought to do" - not the more important question of "what people want to do".
Government agencies are fundamentally public service organizations, staffed by dedicated professionals. However, few face private-sector competition or have identified a mandate to deliver emotionally-resonant products and services to their constituencies.
Consequently, management resources tend to focus on matters like efficiency and cost-effectiveness rather than deep connections, relationships, and motivating attitudes sought by consumer-product organizations.
As a vignette...

In Boulder, among the greenest of cities, conservation managers recently had to dispatch crews to physically change out inefficient light bulbs.

Nobody found a way to motivate consumers to do so on their own.
PES core team members have engaged utilities and durable goods manufacturers like Salt River Project and Whirlpool in enhancing/creating their ability to be customer-centric organizations.
